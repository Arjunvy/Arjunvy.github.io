Thermal Architecture for High-Power Phased-Array/RADAR/EW/SATCOM Development Platform

Designed the thermal management system for a densely populated 16-layer board dissipating 340W of power. Of this, 200W was concentrated in four critical components, while the remaining heat was spread across numerous devices placed throughout the board. Due to non-standard package geometries, direct mounting of FanSinks onto the components wasn’t feasible. I developed a board-wide thermal solution involving a large custom heatspreader plate with FanSinks mounted on top. The plate was supported by strategically placed standoffs that made thermal contact with high-power zones on the PCB. To enable efficient conduction, thermal interfaces were created between the heatspreader plate and the critical components. For components with low junction-to-board thermal resistance, the PCB itself acted as the primary path for heat transfer, conducting heat to the heatspreader touchdowns. This hybrid cooling approach combined direct conduction and forced-air methods to effectively manage heat across the board. The solution brought junction temperatures of all critical parts well below their maximum rated limits while maintaining form factor and acoustic targets — enabling stable, high-performance operation for phased-array, RADAR, EW, and SATCOM applications.

System Architecture & Mechanical Integration

Final mechanical assembly showing thermal integration with component zones.

The design involved a densely populated PCB, presenting challenges in establishing effective thermal paths. I collaborated extensively with layout and firmware engineers to finalize component placement based on thermal dissipation characteristics.

Components with a primary junction–case–ambient thermal path were positioned on the top side of the board to enable direct contact with the heatspreader plate. Conversely, components with a junction–board thermal path were placed on the bottom side in thermally active zones.

These hotspot regions were strategically arranged to facilitate efficient insertion of direct thermal touchdowns from the heatspreader plate to the PCB. Placement strategy was validated through thermal simulations to ensure all component junction temperatures remained below their maximum rated thresholds.

Exploded view highlighting stacked assembly with thermal paths and contacts.

Thermal Management Strategy:
The first stage of thermal management focused on designing the heatspreader plate and selecting an appropriate FanSink. A 1D thermal resistance network was developed to model the primary thermal path—junction to case—of the critical components. This path was segmented into three slabs:

• The top slab represented the junction-to-case thermal resistance.
• The middle slab represented the heat source, and copper was selected here due to its low thermal resistance, ensuring efficient heat spreading.
• The bottom slab accounted for the thermal resistance from junction to board.

This thermal resistance network was instrumental in calculating the required thermal resistance for the FanSink. Four critical components, collectively dissipating 200W of heat, drove the thermal design. The FanSink was selected based on its thermal performance, footprint compatibility, and fan voltage specifications.

Cross-sectional schematic representing the 1D thermal resistance network.

For components where the primary thermal path is junction-to-board, the thermal power was applied as a boundary condition on the corresponding board surface, represented as an outline in the thermal model. This approach ensured accurate representation of heat flow paths in the simulation, contributing to reliable temperature predictions and robust thermal solution design.

Design Simplification for Simulation

Simplified final assembly showing slab representations for junction-to-case components.

Back view of the simplified assembly highlighting the geometry reduction approach.

To enable efficient simulation, key design simplifications were implemented to reduce computational cost, improve meshing robustness, and streamline parametric and optimization studies. As demonstrated in earlier sections, all components with a primary junction-to-case thermal path were modeled using simplified slab geometries that preserve the critical heat transfer characteristics. In contrast, components with junction-to-board thermal paths were represented as surface outlines on the PCB to capture boundary heat flux without introducing unnecessary model complexity. These simplifications ensured the simulation remained focused on the key thermal physics while maintaining accuracy and scalability.

Thermal Simulation Approach and Validation:

Thermal flux for components with a junction-to-case path was applied directly to the middle slab of the simplified model, ensuring realistic heat input at the source. For components with a junction-to-board path, thermal flux was assigned to corresponding surface outlines on the PCB. Additionally, a dedicated surface outline was created on the heatspreader plate to represent the FanSink interface, where thermal flux boundary conditions were applied.

To simulate natural heat dissipation, a standard convective heat transfer coefficient was assigned to all remaining exposed surfaces of the heatspreader and PCB.

The simulation was executed in multiple stages:

  • Stage 1: Simulations were run without any thermal management to observe natural heat spread and baseline thermal performance.
  • Stage 2: A heatspreader with an attached FanSink was introduced, making thermal contact with the critical components. This stage highlighted the board-level hot spots caused by components with junction-to-board heat paths. Additional thermal contacts (“touchdowns”) were added at these hot spots to improve heat spreading.
  • Final Stage: With full thermal management implemented, the simulation showed a dramatic temperature reduction—from approximately 800°C down to 80°C—for the high-power components.
These results were validated with real-world thermocouple measurements at multiple locations on the board, confirming the simulation’s accuracy and the thermal solution's effectiveness.
